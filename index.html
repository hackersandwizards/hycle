hycle

When execution becomes instant, only decisions remain.

Part I

The Manifesto

The Observation

Every few decades, a technology removes a constraint that organizations had built their entire operating model around. Resistance to change was once a function of how long it took to retool a factory. Then of how long it took to ship software. Now, AI agents are removing the execution constraint itself.

When execution accelerates by an order of magnitude, the bottleneck shifts. What remains is the decision: what to build, for whom, and why. Who has the authority? Who has the context? Who is willing to be wrong?

These questions are not new. In manufacturing, logistics, and finance, organizations have wrestled with decision-making speed for decades. What is new is that software organizations — long immunized by talent scarcity and a “part of the solution” self-image — are confronting them for the first time. The special economic conditions that shielded software teams are ending. AI breaks the homogeneity. The bottleneck becomes visible.

In software, the symptoms are vivid: a single developer with an AI agent completes in one day what once took a team two weeks. Backlogs empty. Sprint reviews drown in hundreds of stories no one can absorb. The universal reaction is to abolish Scrum — remove the process, go faster.

This is the wrong response. Not because Scrum was right, but because removing it hides the real problem instead of solving it.

The real problem was never execution speed. It was always the decisions. AI agents removed the comfortable fiction that if only we were faster, everything would work. The water level dropped. The rocks are visible now.

The Name

In 1978, Herbert Simon received the Nobel Prize for a deceptively simple insight: organizations are not systems of people — they are systems of decisions. Every structure, every hierarchy, every process exists to channel decisions. Remove everything else, and decisions are what remain.

In 1971, biophysicist Manfred Eigen described how simple chemical systems organize themselves into self-sustaining cycles — hypercycles — that enable the leap from chemistry to biology. From mechanical to alive. From simple repetition to emergent complexity.

We believe organizations face a similar transition. From process-driven to decision-driven. From coordinated to self-organizing.

We call this transition hycle — the hyper cycle.

German speakers hear heikel — delicate, precarious. That is deliberate. This transition is not comfortable. It challenges power structures, role definitions, and the identity of everyone involved. It is, indeed, heikel.

And yes — hype cycle is in there too. Deliberately. Every few years, a new technology triggers the same predictable curve of inflated expectations, disillusionment, and belated productivity. We named ourselves after that pattern as a reminder: hycle is not about riding the next wave. It is about what remains after the hype has passed.

The Concepts

Three terms carry most of the weight in what follows. We define them here so they mean the same thing throughout.

Decision

We mean primarily the decision about what to build and why — not the decision about how. But the boundary between what and how is itself a decision that most organizations have never made consciously. In practice, we still see management prescribe button labels and screen layouts. The assumption that teams own the how is often a fiction. When we say the decision bottleneck is the real problem, we mean the what and why. When we say decision authority must shift, we mean both.

Strategy

We do not mean vision statements or annual goals. We mean strategy as diagnosis: an honest assessment of the situation, a guiding policy that focuses effort, and a set of coherent actions that follow from both. Strategy in this sense is not a destination — it is the logic that connects observation to action. When we say “management sets strategy,” we mean management owns this diagnostic work, not that management dictates what to build.

Authority

The right to decide is not the same as deciding. Many organizations have formal decision authority that exists on paper but not in practice — or informal authority that operates without acknowledgment. The gap between who may decide and who does decide is often the first symptom of organizational dysfunction. We are interested in actual authority, not declared authority.

What We See

These observations come from practice — from training teams, consulting organizations, and building software with AI agents ourselves.

1 AI agents shift the bottleneck.

Execution speed is no longer the constraint. Decision-making is. In software, one person builds an epic per day. In other domains, AI compresses analysis, drafting, and production to a fraction of their former duration. In all cases, the organization cannot decide what to do next at the pace the work now demands.

2 The decision problem is not new — it is newly visible.

Like lowering the water level in a river reveals the rocks beneath, AI speed exposes organizational dysfunction that was always there. Slow decisions, unclear authority, missing domain knowledge, absent stakeholders. Many industries outside software have known this for decades. The difference is that software organizations are now losing the immunity that talent scarcity and long delivery cycles once provided.

3 Process models designed for throughput optimization break down.

Scrum, SAFe, and similar frameworks optimized for a bottleneck that no longer exists. Sprint planning for 300 stories is not planning. Reviews of work already in production are not reviews. What collapses is the scaffolding around delivery speed — and with it, the shared vocabulary these frameworks provided. What remains worth preserving is the cadence: the discipline of stopping to think.

4 Teams shrink. Responsibility grows.

One- to two-person teams with AI agents carry more domain scope than eight-person cross-functional teams did before. But the organizational structures around them have not adapted.

5 Role boundaries dissolve.

The separation of “developer” and “product manager” loses meaning when execution is instant and the bottleneck is domain understanding. We see these roles converging — toward people who combine deep domain knowledge with the ability to deliver end-to-end and make decisions autonomously. We are not ready to name this role. But the convergence is unmistakable.

6 The coordination layer faces an existential question.

Middle management justified its existence through managing development complexity. That complexity is evaporating. But the challenge extends beyond middle management. Boards, founders, and executives face their own reckoning: when teams can move faster than the hierarchy can direct them, what is the role of leadership?

7 Organizations regress under pressure.

In times of high uncertainty, leaders reach for central control — not because the situation demands it, but because the need to feel effective disguises itself as the need to lead. Organizations that had developed toward distributed decision-making get actively reset to command-and-control by their own founders. The drivers are identity, role confusion, and unexamined certainty — not evidence. Progression through the maturity levels is not monotonic.

What We Believe

We do not have answers. We have hypotheses. They come from practice, not theory. We hold them loosely and test them rigorously.

H1 Organizations must reorganize around decisions, not around execution processes.

The fundamental unit of organizational value is a decision — not a feature, not a story, not a sprint goal, not a deliverable.

H2 Organizational maturity is determined by where decisions are made — not by how fast work is shipped.

Speed without decision authority produces faster waste.

H3 Autonomy without sharpness produces faster ambiguity.

Distributing decision authority solves nothing if the decisions themselves remain diluted. Real decisions require contrast: genuine alternatives, a real trade-off, and someone willing to stand behind the consequence.

H4 Decisions that cross team boundaries need explicit ownership at the right level.

A decision must be owned where it first affects more than one entity. Without this, team-level autonomy creates local optimization at the cost of organizational coherence. Ownership level depends on the reach of the consequence, not on hierarchy.

H5 Frustration is a signal, not a problem.

The discomfort teams feel when AI speed meets organizational inertia is the indicator of where to intervene next. Do not hide it. Follow it.

H6 The path is iterative and bottom-up.

There is no master plan for this transition. You navigate from bottleneck to bottleneck. Each one reveals the next.

H7 The default shifts from instruction to intent to autonomy.

Autonomy — for teams and for AI agents — must be earned through consistent delivery, not declared by management. You cannot skip levels. There are no shortcuts.

H8 The opportunity is growth, not reduction.

AI that accelerates execution unlocks a choice: do the same with fewer people, or do fundamentally more with the same people. The first is a one-time saving. The second is a competitive advantage that compounds.

What hycle Is Not

hycle is not a new Scrum. It is not a process framework with ceremonies, roles, and artifacts to implement on Monday morning.

hycle is not a technology framework. It does not prescribe tools, architectures, or AI platforms.

hycle is not against Agile. The values of the Agile Manifesto still hold. The process models built on top of them do not — not at this speed.

hycle is not prescriptive. It provides principles, diagnosis, and reference patterns. The operationalization must be developed for each context — by the people and AI agents working in that context.

hycle is not finished. It is a set of observations and hypotheses from practice, offered as an invitation to collective sense-making.

Part II

The Maturity Model

The hycle maturity model describes organizational maturity along two co-evolving dimensions:

Decision Authority Where are decisions made?
Human-AI Trust How much autonomy have teams earned with their AI agents?

These dimensions are not independent. You cannot advance on one without advancing on the other. An organization that grants AI agents full autonomy without team-level decision authority will build the wrong thing faster. A team with full decision authority but no trust in their AI tools will be too slow to use it.

The transition between levels is not linear. Organizations do not “implement Level 3.” They earn it — through practice, through pain, through honest confrontation with their own decision-making structures. You cannot skip levels. There are no shortcuts. And progression is not guaranteed — under pressure, organizations routinely regress to earlier levels. The pull toward central control is strong, especially when uncertainty is high.

Level 0 Acceleration “Tell me what to build” Observed
Decision Authority: Unchanged. Management decides. Teams execute.
Human-AI Trust: Low. Every output is reviewed. Every step is dictated.
What happens: AI is bolted onto existing processes. Backlogs empty. Teams feel fast but frustrated. The typical reaction — abolishing Scrum — makes the problem invisible, not solved.
Decision quality perceived by teams: Opaque. Teams cannot tell whether requirements were validated as hypotheses or whether the decision process above them is evidence-based. They doubt that what they are told to build will lead to meaningful outcomes. Motivation erodes.
What it takes to move beyond: Someone must name the real bottleneck. The frustration teams feel is not about tools or process — it is about decisions. Until this is said out loud, the organization will keep optimizing for speed.

You are here if: Teams complain about having nothing to do.

Level 1 Visibility “I see the real problem” Observed
Decision Authority: Awareness. Teams recognize the decision gap but cannot close it.
Human-AI Trust: Growing. AI suggests approaches. Humans still decide everything.
What happens: The realization sets in that execution speed was never the bottleneck. Product owners cannot specify fast enough. Questions pile up faster than answers. Organizational walls become the dominant impediment.
Decision quality perceived by teams: Questioned. Teams see that decisions reaching them are slow, fragmented, or contradictory. They start asking where decisions come from and whether the right people are making them.
What it takes to move beyond: The decision problem must become visible not just to teams but to leadership. Organizations that progress begin to map their critical decisions — who owns them, where the bottlenecks are, where AI supports and where it does not. This is not a framework to adopt but a diagnostic exercise: making the invisible architecture of decisions explicit.

You are here if: Frustration points at the organization, not at the tools.

Level 2 Convergence “I intend to...” Hypothesized
Decision Authority: Shifting. Teams propose directions. The gap between “developer” and “product person” narrows. Domain expertise moves into the team — not as an external input, but as a core capability.
Human-AI Trust: Earned. AI presents intent before acting. Thin vertical slices. Rapid iteration. Trust calibrated through consistent delivery.
What happens: Discovery skills become critical. Domain understanding outweighs technical specialization. The need for new skills emerges organically — not from a training plan, but from necessity. Teams that cannot internalize domain knowledge stall at this level.
Decision quality perceived by teams: Improving. Teams experience decisions as more grounded because they participate in shaping them. The what and the why become visible. Feedback from customers and data begins to inform decisions directly, not through intermediaries.
What it takes to move beyond: Teams develop explicit agreements about what decisions AI agents can make autonomously and what requires human judgment — calibrated per workflow, adjusted based on outcomes. The form varies: some teams use formal trust contracts, others evolve informal norms. The mechanism matters less than the explicitness. Discovery must become a team capability, not a separate function.

You are here if: Teams ask “What should we build?” more often than “How should we build it?”

Level 3 Autonomy “I’ve been doing...” Hypothesized
Decision Authority: Team-level. Feedback loops connect directly to customers and data. Management owns the diagnostic work — assessing the situation, setting guiding policies, ensuring coherence across teams. Teams own execution and domain decisions within that frame.
Human-AI Trust: High. Autonomous execution on earned trust. The human holds direction, taste, and judgment. The AI holds continuity, detail, and execution.
What happens: Review becomes reflection. Coordination overhead drops. Teams operate as self-sufficient units with full domain responsibility.
Decision quality perceived by teams: High. Teams trust the strategic frame they operate in because they can trace it back to diagnosis, not opinion. When management decisions do not meet this standard, teams have the standing and the language to push back. The feedback loop works in both directions.
What it takes to move beyond: The organizational boundary between teams and strategy must dissolve without losing coherence. This requires decision-making structures that emerge from the work itself rather than being imposed from above. Few organizations have reached this point. The prerequisites are not yet fully understood.

You are here if: Decisions emerge from customer need, not from hierarchy.

Level 4 Self-Organization “The Hypercycle” Hypothesized
Decision Authority: Emergent. The system organizes itself.
Human-AI Trust: Beyond individual calibration. Systems of humans and agents form self-sustaining feedback loops.
What happens: This is a hypothesis. We see early signals — automated feedback loops that deploy and validate autonomously, customer behavior that directly drives development priority. But no organization has fully realized this level.
Decision quality perceived by teams: The distinction between “team” and “organization” blurs. Decision quality is not perceived — it is measured, continuously, by the system itself.

You are here if: We do not know yet. This is where we need your experience.

What does it take to reach Level 4?

We have early hypotheses. We are testing them with real organizations. This is the question we most need help answering.

Part III

The Framework

The manifesto defines what we observe and believe. The maturity model provides a diagnostic lens. But observation and diagnosis alone do not change organizations. The framework is the bridge to practice.

This is deliberately not a prescriptive methodology. hycle provides reference patterns — ways to think about operationalization, not a recipe to follow. The concrete practices must be developed for each organizational context, by the people and AI agents working in that context. What follows are the building blocks we consider essential.

The Roles

When execution is no longer the bottleneck, the division between “the person who decides what to build” and “the person who builds it” stops making sense. We see these roles converging — toward people who combine domain expertise, discovery capability, and end-to-end delivery in a single responsibility.

We are not ready to name this role or define its boundaries. But the convergence is real, it is accelerating, and it changes what organizations need from the people inside them.

The Language

Process frameworks like Scrum gave organizations more than a process — they gave them a shared vocabulary. Sprint, backlog, retrospective, product owner: these terms allowed people across roles and levels to coordinate with precision. As these frameworks lose their relevance, the vocabulary goes with them.

A decision-driven organization needs its own shared language — terms for talking about decisions, authority, trust, and autonomy that work for both human-to-human and human-to-AI communication. The concepts defined in the manifesto are a starting point. The operative vocabulary for daily use is what this section will provide.
