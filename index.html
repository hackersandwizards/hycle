hycle

When execution becomes instant, only decisions remain.

Part I

The Manifesto

The Observation

Every few decades, a technology removes a constraint that organizations built their operating model around. AI agents are removing the execution constraint itself.

When execution accelerates by an order of magnitude, the bottleneck shifts to decisions: what to build, for whom, and why. This is not new — other industries have known it for decades. Software organizations are confronting it for the first time.

The symptoms are vivid: one developer completes in a day what took a team two weeks. Backlogs empty. The universal reaction is to abolish Scrum. This hides the problem instead of solving it.

The real problem was never execution speed. It was always decisions. The water level dropped. The rocks are visible now.

The Name

Herbert Simon (1978): organizations are not systems of people — they are systems of decisions.

Manfred Eigen (1971): simple systems organize into self-sustaining cycles — hypercycles — enabling the leap from mechanical to alive.

We believe organizations face a similar transition. From process-driven to decision-driven. We call it hycle — the hyper cycle.

German speakers hear heikel — delicate, precarious. That is deliberate.

And yes — hype cycle is in there too. A reminder that hycle is about what remains after the hype has passed.

One name we rejected: MURCS — Scrum spelled backwards. In German that reads as Murks: botched work. Heikel was the honest choice.

The Concepts

Decision

The what and why, not the how. But where that boundary sits is itself a decision most organizations never consciously make — managers still prescribe button labels.

Strategy

Not vision statements. Diagnosis: an honest assessment of the situation, a guiding policy that focuses effort, and coherent actions that follow from both.

Authority

Not who may decide, but who does. The gap between formal and actual authority is often the first symptom — and the hardest to name.

Agency

Authority gives you a seat at the table; agency is whether your presence at it matters. AI agents extend agency or replace it — the fault line of decision-driven organizations.

What We See

1 AI agents shift the bottleneck.

Execution speed is no longer the constraint. Decision-making is. Organizations cannot decide at the pace the work now demands.

2 The decision problem is not new — it is newly visible.

AI speed exposes dysfunction that was always there: slow decisions, unclear authority, absent stakeholders.

3 Process models invert.

The new Scrum, not the new Agile. Scrum optimized for an execution bottleneck that no longer exists. The cadence still serves humans — but its content inverts.

4 Teams shrink. Responsibility grows.

One-person teams carry more scope than eight-person teams did. Organizational structures have not adapted.

5 Role boundaries dissolve.

Developer and product manager converge. The new role combines domain knowledge, end-to-end delivery, and decision-making.

6 The coordination layer faces an existential question.

When teams move faster than hierarchy can direct them, the role of leadership cannot stay the same. What it becomes — frame, diagnosis, coherence — is still open.

7 Organizations regress under pressure.

The need to feel effective disguises itself as the need to lead. Leaders recentralize out of identity and habit, not evidence. Progression is not monotonic.

What We Believe

We do not have answers. We have hypotheses. Held loosely, tested rigorously.

H1 Reorganize around decisions, not execution processes.

The fundamental unit of organizational value is a decision — not a feature, not a deliverable.

H2 Maturity is where decisions are made, not how fast work ships.

Speed without authority produces faster waste.

H3 Autonomy without sharpness produces faster ambiguity.

Real decisions require genuine alternatives, a real trade-off, and someone willing to own the consequence.

H4 Cross-boundary decisions need explicit ownership.

Owned where they first affect more than one entity. Ownership depends on reach, not hierarchy.

H5 Frustration is a signal, not a problem.

Follow it.

H6 The path is iterative and bottom-up.

No master plan. Bottleneck to bottleneck.

H7 From instruction to intent to autonomy and agency.

Earned, not declared. No shortcuts.

H8 The opportunity is growth, not reduction.

Do fundamentally more with the same people. That compounds.

What hycle Is Not

Not a Monday morning rollout. Scrum’s events and artifacts mostly survive. What inverts is their content — and content cannot be declared from the top.

Not a technology framework. No prescribed tools or platforms.

Not against Agile. The values hold. The process models invert.

Not prescriptive. Principles, diagnosis, reference patterns. The operationalization is yours.

Not finished. An invitation to collective sense-making.

Part II

The Maturity Model

Decision Authority Where are decisions made?
Human-AI Trust How much autonomy and agency have teams earned with their AI agents?

These dimensions co-evolve. You cannot advance on one without advancing on the other. Full AI autonomy without team-level decision authority builds the wrong thing faster. Full decision authority without trust in AI tools is too slow to use.

Levels are earned, not implemented. Progression is not guaranteed. Under pressure, organizations regress.

These are not steps to climb. Each level has costs. Some organizations operate well at Level 1 forever. Level 4 is not the goal — fit is.

Level 0 Acceleration “Tell me what to build” Observed
Decision Authority: Unchanged. Management decides. Teams execute.
Human-AI Trust: Low. Every output reviewed. Every step dictated.
What happens: AI bolted onto existing processes. Backlogs empty. The reaction — abolishing Scrum — hides the problem.
Decision quality perceived by teams: Opaque. Teams cannot tell whether requirements were validated. Motivation erodes.
What it costs: Speed feels normal. The decision gap stays invisible. The cost is paid in retention and morale.
Move beyond: Name the real bottleneck. It is decisions, not speed.

Signal: Teams complain about having nothing to do.

Level 1 Visibility “I see the real problem” Observed
Decision Authority: Awareness. Teams recognize the decision gap but cannot close it.
Human-AI Trust: Growing. AI suggests. Humans still decide everything.
What happens: Product owners cannot specify fast enough. Organizational walls become the dominant impediment.
Decision quality perceived by teams: Questioned. Decisions reaching teams are slow, fragmented, or contradictory.
What it costs: Visibility without authority creates frustration. People see what is broken and cannot fix it.
Move beyond: Map critical decisions. Make the invisible architecture explicit. A diagnostic exercise, not a framework.

Signal: Frustration points at the organization, not at the tools.

Level 2 Convergence “I intend to...” Hypothesized
Decision Authority: Shifting. Teams propose directions. Domain expertise becomes a core team capability.
Human-AI Trust: Earned. AI presents intent before acting. Trust calibrated through delivery.
What happens: Discovery skills outweigh technical specialization. Teams shape decisions. The what and why become visible.
Decision quality perceived by teams: Improving. Teams participate in shaping decisions. Feedback from customers informs directly.
What it costs: Coordination overhead grows. Teams shape decisions but still negotiate alignment. Being decentralized is real work.
Move beyond: Explicit agreements on AI autonomy per workflow. Discovery as team capability, not separate function.

Signal: Teams ask “What should we build?” more than “How?”

Level 3 Autonomy “I’ve been doing...” Hypothesized
Decision Authority: Team-level. Management owns diagnosis and coherence. Teams own domain decisions.
Human-AI Trust: High. Human holds direction and judgment. AI holds continuity and detail.
What happens: Review becomes reflection. Teams operate as self-sufficient units with full domain responsibility.
Decision quality perceived by teams: High. Strategic frame traceable to diagnosis, not opinion. Feedback runs both directions.
What it costs: Cohesion depends on judgment, not process. There is nowhere to hide. Strong individuals can drift; weak ones can stall.
Move beyond: Decision structures must emerge from work, not be imposed. Prerequisites not yet understood.

Signal: Decisions emerge from customer need, not hierarchy.

Level 4 Self-Organization “The hyper cycle” Hypothesized
Decision Authority: Emergent. The system organizes itself.
Human-AI Trust: Beyond individual calibration. Self-sustaining feedback loops.
What happens: No organization has fully realized this level.
Decision quality perceived by teams: Measured by the system, not perceived by teams.
What it costs: Unknown. We do not have observations to ground a cost claim.

Signal: We do not know yet. This is where we need your experience.

Part III

The Inversion

The manifesto observes. The model diagnoses. This part shows what inverts.

The values hold. The rituals invert. What follows is a sketch — held loosely, tested rigorously — not a method.

Three Cadences

When execution becomes instant, one cadence is no longer enough. Three rhythms operate at once, and they do not align.

The technical loop

Seconds to minutes. Agent-driven. We call this the factory — the model proposes, runs, fails, corrects, retries, and converges on its own. No human ritual lives at this layer; trying to put one there is what made dailies feel broken.

The human cadence

A day, a week. Anchored in what humans can hold in their heads, not in what the system produces. Stable across contexts because we did not change — the tools did. This is where coordination, judgment, and trust are negotiated.

The business cadence

A quarter, a year. Set by markets, regulation, ownership, contracts. Not negotiable from inside the team. Hyper cycles do not repeal annual reports.

The work of organizing is making these three cadences meet without any one of them dictating to the others.

Three Shifts

The backlog inverts.

What you can resolve in the pair, you build — immediately, in the loop. What you cannot resolve, you write down. The backlog stops being a queue of features and becomes a register of unanswered questions. Building is cheap. Knowing what to build is the bottleneck.

The pair inverts.

Engineers no longer pair on the code. The agent does that. The pair that matters now closes the gap between intent and what gets built — the builder next to the person who lives the problem. When that pair sits together, decisions land in seconds. When it does not, the work stalls — faster than before, and more visibly.

Both retros invert.

The Daily Scrum was a status check on humans. With agents in the loop, it becomes a short retro on yesterday’s agent runs — what to tune, what to fix. The Sprint Retrospective becomes a cooldown — a longer cycle to clean up the code, prune the backlog, and resolve the assumptions left open during fast work. Two retros, two cadences, two scopes.

The shifts compound. None of them holds without the others.

What Inverts

Scrum’s events and artifacts do not disappear. Their content turns inside out.

ElementBeforeAfter
SprintTwo-week timeboxPer-ticket loop
Sprint PlanningDecide upfront what fits the sprintJust-in-time, in the factory
Daily ScrumStatus sync among humansRetro on yesterday’s agent runs
Sprint ReviewDemo to stakeholders at the endCollapses into the pair — the stakeholder is there as the work happens
Sprint RetrospectiveBi-weekly improvement eventCooldown — clean up code, backlog, and assumptions
Product BacklogFeatures queued to buildAssumptions queued to verify
IncrementOne shippable result per sprintThe product ships continuously; the factory is what increments

What Does Not Invert

The Agile values. Individuals, working software, customer collaboration, response to change.

Trunk-based development. Small steps, fast feedback, continuous integration.

Building with the people who use it. In the smallest steps that teach you something.

Without these, the inversion does not hold — it accelerates the wrong thing.
